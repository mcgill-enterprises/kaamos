# Worldbuilding: Kaamos

> *The polar night. The period of total darkness that eventually breaks.*

This document captures the lore, naming conventions, and design decisions behind the
Kaamos universe. It is a living document — add to it as the world grows.

---

## Literary sources

The naming palette draws from four literary traditions, each contributing a distinct
emotional register. Names are not chosen arbitrarily — each carries the weight of its source.

### Eino Leino — *Helkavirsiä* (1903, 1916)
The Whitsun Songs. Leino reached back past Christianity into pre-Christian Finnish oral
tradition, filtering it through late-Romantic symbolism and a profound personal darkness.
The poems feel incanted rather than written. Fate is accepted rather than fought.
The Finnish tradition has almost none of the heroic defiance found in Norse myth —
you walk toward the dark with a kind of terrible clarity.

Key work: *Tumma* ("The Dark One") — the poem that inspired Insomnium's
*In the Groves of Death*.

### Kaarlo Sarkia
A quieter, more personal grief than Leino. Sarkia's imagery — longing, fading light,
autumn — contributes the names that carry *ikävä*: the untranslatable Finnish word
for deep longing, homesickness, and grief intertwined.

Key influence: *Above the Weeping World* (Insomnium).

### Edgar Allan Poe
English-language darkness that contrasts deliberately with the Finnish names.
Poe contributes the stations and regions that feel like places of slow collapse —
things that were once significant and are now decaying beautifully.

Key works: *Ulalume*, *The Fall of the House of Usher*, *Eldorado*.

### Friedrich Hölderlin
Fallen grandeur. Hölderlin's late fragmented poems have a cosmic, dissociated quality
that maps to the void regions — vast, broken, once-great things.

Key works: *Hyperion*, *Der Tod des Empedokles* (unfinished), *Patmos*.

## Naming conventions

- **All identifiers and instructions are English.** Finnish and literary names are
  display names and lore only.
- **Ship names** are vessels of fate — they carry the living through the dark,
  and sometimes don't return.
- **Station names** are fixed points in the void — places of brief refuge, not safety.
- **Sector names** are regions with their own character — some navigable, some not.
- **Faction names** reflect their relationship to loss, light, and the void.

## The Helka Expanse

The primary sector. The oldest part of the galaxy — not ancient in the sense of ruins,
but ancient in the sense that something has always been happening here that humans
don't fully understand.

The routes are well-traveled. The reasons why ships sometimes don't arrive are never
fully explained. Not hostile. Not safe. Indifferent in the specifically Finnish way —
the void doesn't wish you harm, it simply doesn't notice you.

**Navigation in the Helka Expanse is done by the old songs.** Not literally — ships
use standard navigation — but pilots who fly the Expanse long enough develop a practice
of listening. There are known interference patterns that experienced pilots learn to read
like weather. They don't talk about this to outsiders.

**The Expanse has no landmarks, only thresholds.** You don't navigate *to* a place
in the Helka Expanse, you navigate *through* a series of conditions. The stations are
positioned at these thresholds, not at arbitrary coordinates.

**Ships that disappear in the Expanse are said to have "gone into the song."**
This is not considered a tragedy by the people who live and work there.
It is considered an ending, which is different.

## Hiljaisuus — The Silence

The deep void sector. The galaxy's chokepoint.

When Hiljaisuus is destabilized, fuel flow across the entire Helka Expanse is choked.
Tuonela Station and Manala Depot begin drawing down reserves. Pohjan Neito begins
rationing. Fuel prices at Ikävä Anchorage spike. Ships are stranded mid-route.

This is the economic heart of the game's volatility model — a single sector whose
stability determines the price of survival everywhere else.

---

## Economic model — fuel price volatility

Modeled respectfully on real-world petroleum market dynamics:
chokepoint disruption, inventory drawdown, faction rationing, and the compounding
effect of duration over severity.

The `galaxy.sectors.alerts` Kafka topic is the price signal backbone.
A `SectorFuelPriceUpdated` event fires when a station recalculates fuel price based on:

- Current reserve levels at that station
- Whether the route through Hiljaisuus is open or disrupted
- Faction control of nearby supply nodes
- Time since last resupply convoy

Price shocks propagate through the event backbone in real time.
Ships reroute. Stations ration. The *Kaipaus* takes the long way around.
